Chapter 1 · Context

Where We Are

Every organization passes through predictable phases of growth. Understanding which phase TreviPay is in — and which is next — is the foundation for everything we'll discuss today.

Greiner's Growth Model

Organizations don't just scale — they evolve

Each phase has its own operating logic. And each phase ends in a crisis that forces a shift to the next.

Phase 3 · Past
Delegation
  • Execution driven from the top
  • Speed through hierarchy
  • Leaders as decision-makers
  • Functional accountability
▲ We are here
Phase 4 · Now
Coordination
  • Common systems & standards
  • Shared language across functions
  • Leaders as system designers
  • Horizontal coordination
Phase 5 · Direction
Collaboration
  • Cross-functional teams
  • Peer accountability
  • Shared enterprise outcomes
  • Leaders as culture keepers
Coordination has two dimensions

TreviPay has built one. Not both.

Coordination isn't just top-down systems and planning cycles. It also requires lateral structures — and those are largely absent.

✓ Present
Vertical Coordination

Frameworks, reporting cycles, standards — flowing downward from ELT. Workday, the competency framework, the behavioral profile.

✗ Absent
Horizontal Coordination

Cross-functional squads, shared ownership, solving problems at the source. These structures don't yet exist at lower levels.

→

The result: problems escalate upward instead of being resolved laterally. The ELT becomes the coordination mechanism rather than the designer of one.

The implication

You can't skip Coordination
to get to Collaboration

What looks like Collaboration

ELT operates with open debate and shared norms

Leaders model peer accountability at the top

Rhetoric rewards breaking silos

What's actually needed first

Horizontal coordination mechanisms below ELT

Cross-functional squads solving at the source

Systems that reward coordination behavior

The organization can't skip the phase. The ELT can aspire to Phase 5 — but until Phase 4 is complete, the aspiration creates confusion, not progress.

Chapter 2 · Diagnosis

The Core Tension

TreviPay has a clear aspiration. The problem is not the aspiration — it's that the operating system hasn't caught up to it.

The gap between intention and reality

This is not a commitment problem

What we intend
Shared ownership
Enterprise outcomes

The gap
The system rewards coordination behavior while the aspiration points toward collaboration.
The legitimacy gap

When aspiration outpaces structure

When the ELT models collaborative norms while the organization's formal systems still reward coordination behavior, it creates a gap that middle managers feel most acutely.

⬆️

Decisions move upward

Cross-functional resolution happens at ELT, not through defined roles and processes below.

❓

Unclear ownership below ELT

Limited clarity on how decisions should be made at the VP and Director layers.

🔄

Trade-offs deferred

Reluctance to fully define ownership means ambiguity compounds over time.

The Trust Equation

Trust is the mechanism of Collaboration

The shift from Coordination to Collaboration requires something that cannot be mandated: peer-to-peer trust. This team's current trust profile reveals exactly where the work is.

Trust = (C + R + I) ÷ Self-Orientation

✓ High Credibility

Trusted for expertise. Every ELT member earns this.

✓ High Reliability

Dependable within functional areas. Consistent track record.

⚠ Self-Orientation drag

Above-average scores are the primary drag on trustworthiness as a peer group.

Watch-out

Peer trust has relied on a moderating presence to broker alignment — rather than being built directly between peers.

Collaboration requires trust that works without a moderator in the room — and that's exactly what the six behaviors in Chapter 4 are designed to build.

Chapter 3 · The Shift

What Has to Change

Closing the gap doesn't require a new strategy. It requires the ELT to shift its primary activity — from driving execution to designing the system that enables execution.

From execution to architecture

The behaviors that created success
prevent scale

Driving execution is what got everyone in this room to where they are. But at enterprise scale, execution-focused leaders become bottlenecks.

Driving execution directly
→
Designing the system that enables execution
Resolving cross-functional conflicts
→
Defining how conflicts should be resolved
Maintaining alignment through involvement
→
Maintaining alignment through operating discipline
Being the mechanism for coordination
→
Reinforcing the mechanisms for coordination
What this unlocks

Speed at the right level

When the ELT stops being the coordination mechanism and starts designing one, the whole organization moves faster.

Today

Decisions wait for ELT availability

Cross-functional problems escalate upward

The org moves at ELT's pace

→
With the operating system in place

Decisions made at the right level

Problems solved at the source

The org moves at the speed it's capable of

🔑

The prerequisite: This shift requires peer-to-peer trust. Without it, handing off coordination creates chaos. The six behaviors in Chapter 4 are what make the trust operational.

Chapter 4 · The Behaviors

The Six Behaviors

Not values. Not aspirations. Operating expectations — observable, enforceable, and non-negotiable at this level of the organization.

Six enterprise operating behaviors

Each one has a structural purpose

🎯
01
Clear Decision Ownership
Every decision has a named owner. Accountability for process, outcome, and communication.
🤝
02
Peer-to-Peer Resolution
Cross-functional issues resolved directly with peers — escalations are rare and reserved for enterprise-critical issues.
🏛
03
Enterprise-First Trade-offs
Decisions explicitly identify trade-offs. Enterprise outcomes over functional optimization.
✅
04
Decision Commitment
Once decided, the team aligns and executes — even if they initially disagreed.
🔗
05
Early Cross-Functional Engagement
Relevant stakeholders included early — before direction is set, not after.
📣
06
Shared Accountability
Misalignment addressed directly, in real time. Silence is not alignment.
Why these six — not others

Each behavior closes a specific gap

These weren't chosen as values. They were chosen because they directly address the three patterns sustaining the legitimacy gap.

Pattern 1
Decisions move upward

Behaviors 01, 02, and 04 directly address this — by requiring named owners, peer resolution, and commitment to outcomes.

Pattern 2
Unclear ownership below ELT

Behavior 03 addresses this directly — by requiring trade-offs to be made explicit and enterprise outcomes to be named.

Pattern 3
Trade-offs deferred

Behaviors 05 and 06 close this — by requiring early engagement and real-time accountability for misalignment.

What comes next: In the workshop, your team will define what each behavior actually looks like in TreviPay's context — and what it doesn't. Your definitions, not prescribed ones.

Chapter 5 · The Dir+ Standard

From Functional Leader
to Enterprise Co-Owner

The ELT can't lead differently without the layer below evolving alongside. This chapter is about what that evolution looks like — and what we're asking Dir+ leaders to become.

The core ask

This is an identity shift — not a skills gap

The most significant leadership transition in a career is when a functional leader stops optimizing for their domain and starts co-owning enterprise outcomes. TreviPay has never named that shift clearly. This is us naming it.

🏛

Your function is your domain — not your identity

Dir+ leaders are expected to advocate for enterprise outcomes first, and bring their functional expertise to that work — not the other way around.

🤝

Peer accountability is part of the job

Naming misalignment directly — with the person, not around them — is an operating expectation at this level, not a personality trait.

📈

The measure changes

A Dir+ leader who delivers functional results but leaves no stronger leaders behind — and whose team creates escalation dependency — has not fully done the job.

What changes in practice

Six behavioral shifts — observable and specific

Resolving cross-functional conflicts by pulling in the ELT
→
Working it out directly with the relevant peer first
Optimizing for your function's metrics and resource needs
→
Naming trade-offs explicitly and prioritizing the enterprise outcome
Engaging peers after a recommendation is already formed
→
Bringing relevant functions in before direction is set
Cascading enterprise decisions with personal distance or hedging
→
Cascading with fidelity — disagreement belongs in the room, not after it
Staying quiet on misalignment to preserve relationships
→
Naming it directly — with the person, not around them
Developing your own team as a management task
→
Owning the leadership pipeline as a strategic obligation

Chapter 6 · Accountability Mechanisms

What Makes the
Change Real

We've agreed on what needs to change and what that requires of Dir+ leaders. This chapter names what makes it stick — the structural mechanisms that translate agreement into accountability.

The honest assessment

We haven't done a good job of
changing behavior without structure

This is not a critique. It's a pattern that's true of every organization at this stage — and being honest about it is what makes the accountability mechanisms credible rather than punitive.

📋

Stated expectations without measurement don't change behavior

Dir+ leaders need to know what the bar is before they're measured against it. These mechanisms give clarity — not surprise.

🔗

How we reward shapes behavior more reliably than culture statements

Aligning rewards to behavioral expectations signals that this is a business priority — not a People & Culture initiative.

⚖️

Accountability without clarity is unfair. Clarity without accountability is ineffective.

These two mechanisms together close both gaps simultaneously.

Two accountability levers

Structural — not personal

Both mechanisms treat behavioral expectations as a business standard — the same way financial performance targets are treated. This is how trust in the system gets built.

Mechanism 01
Performance Cycle Linkage
Enterprise operating behaviors become a formal evaluation component for all Dir+ leaders at the next performance cycle.

The expectation begins now — not at the next review

This is a launch, not a development runway

Perfection is not the goal — put these behaviors into practice, don't develop them

Mechanism 02
Rewards Alignment
How we reward Dir+ leaders will reflect what we now expect of them — behavioral accountability alongside functional outcomes.

Rewards signal what the organization actually values

Behavioral expectations without rewards alignment create mixed signals

How we lead is as important as what we deliver

What rewards alignment means in practice

The bonus policy is changing

This is a business decision — not a P&C initiative. Behavioral expectations at the executive level will be formally linked to how Dir+ leaders are compensated. The CEO owns this change.

📎

Enterprise operating behaviors become a bonus component

The six behaviors agreed today — and the Dir+ expectations the ELT co-designed — will be factored into the next compensation cycle alongside functional performance.

🎯

This applies at the Dir+ level — not just the executive layer

The expectation cascades to the leaders this room is now sponsoring. The standard we set today is the standard they'll be measured against.

📣

Leaders deserve to know the bar before they're measured against it

The expectation begins now. The rollout mid-June is not a surprise — it's the signal that the work has already started.

This isn't about compliance. It's about making the signal clear enough that the right behavior becomes the rational choice.

How performance is assessed

Dir+ assessed as a stand-alone group — not within functions

Cross-functional calibration means a Director in one function is measured against the same standard as a Director in any other. The enterprise standard applies equally — not locally.

Performance Distribution
Mandatory 5/10/50/20/10 framework

Rising Bar
The bar goes up each year
Yesterday's "meets expectations" becomes tomorrow's baseline. Every cycle, the threshold for each tier rises — forcing continuous elevation of the Dir+ population.
Evidence-Based Ratings
Gut feel is not a rating
Every Dir+ rating requires evidence demonstrating performance against role expectations and prior year performance. Written rationale retained in Workday.
Acceptable Reward Ranges
Clear guidelines per performance level
Defined award ranges for merit and bonus by rating tier. Justification required for any variance. Any distribution where all awards fall within 10% of each other is automatically flagged by P&C.
Checks & balances

Preventing inflation and compression

Two sets of guardrails — one for rating integrity, one for award differentiation. Both are structural, not advisory.

📐 Rating Integrity
Preventing artificial inflation & compression
1
Mandatory distribution curve
Ratings must reflect a spread consistent with the 5/10/50/20/10 framework.
2
Evidence-based ratings
Every Dir+ rating requires documented evidence. Gut feel is not a rating.
3
PCBP review before calibration
PCBPs discuss all ratings with SVP prior to calibration and push back on bias.
4
Stand-alone Dir+ calibration
Dir+ population assessed as its own group — cross-functional focus on level expectations and how results are achieved.
🎯 Award Differentiation
Preventing peanut-buttering of the pool
1
Acceptable reward ranges by rating
Clear guidelines for merit and bonus by tier. Justification required for any variance.
2
Named rationale for outliers
Written justification required for every individual in the top and bottom tier. Retained in Workday.
3
Variance floor
Any distribution where all awards fall within 10% of each other is automatically flagged by P&C before submission.
4
Rating-to-award alignment check
P&C validates award levels are directionally consistent with ratings. Misalignment requires written explanation.
Executive calibration · Director+ only

ELT reviews every Dir+ rating and award

Before any Director+ outcome is finalized — full ELT review, cross-functional visibility, collective sign-off. Final ratings and awards approved by CEO.

The four steps
1
Each executive presents their Dir+ ratings and award distribution with named rationale
2
Peer executives may challenge any rating or award where cross-functional evidence contradicts it
3
P&C flags any distribution failing to meet guidelines before the session — no flat distributions enter unchallenged
4
Final ratings and awards approved by CEO — adjusted to meet guidelines if the team fails to do so on their own
Scope
Director & Above · FY 2026
100% Discretionary Bonus model. Executive Calibration Required.
Why this matters
Cross-functional visibility means a Chief can see — and challenge — how a Director in another function is being rated. Functional protection of underperformers becomes visible and accountable.
Differentiation is a leadership act.
The process must make it unavoidable.
